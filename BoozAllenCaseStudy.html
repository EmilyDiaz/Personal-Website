Case studies for Booz Allen, 2022-Present

Software

This proprietary case management software* is used to track the lifecycle of a case starting at intake and then throughout different organizational queues as new actions are added to the case. Scope of enhancements vary given the numerous amount of different users that utilze & depend on this software on a daily basis.

*Further details on this project cannot be discuss due to NDA. Any information presented has been generalized and some parts have even been redacted.

Choose a problem statement below for more information:

Problem 1

Tracking veteran correspondence

Current software does not allow for tracking of mail that is crucial for completing the lifecycle of cases. Mail must be managed through another, external software, which hinders the users' productivity as well as does not allow for in depth tracking of the mail's life cycle. Proper reporting needs were not being met for the mail team, which is crucial for auditing as well as performance reviews.

Problem 2

Issue approval process

Without proper guardrails in place, any user could change issues and specific details of an issue, which could lead to a case being misrouted or mismanaged as well as incorrect reporting.

Problem 3

Decision Evaluations

After reviewing a case, judges will submit an evaluation of the attorney's performance on the case. This evaluation is currently not available post-submission, which prevents judges and other authorized users from correcting or updating any feedback that might impact an attorney's pay and work opportunities.

Challenges faced

  • MVP constraints were not established during the early solutioning phases with the client
  • Gathering of business requirements and designs progressed for nearly a year before ready to pass onto development, making communication stale in some areas
  • My first time leading a project made this a learn as you go situation

Solution

Intergrate mail tracking and management into the case manangement software, as well as allow for relationships to be established between mail and their corresponding cases.

Mapping out the user flow

Our team at Booz Allen reached out to the mail team stakeholders for two rounds of demonstrations. Both demonstrations were conducted on the external software program the mail team used for intaking their mail and were run in addition to requirement gathering meetings.

We mapped out a rough user flow of how this external program worked and then worked out how this new flow would fit into the case management program. Our case managment program would recieve mail from the mail team's software on a defined schedule and place it in assignment queues.

Three major components were identified in the user flow:

Component One

Component One focuses on the communication between the two applications and being able to read the mail in the case management software.

Mail would be pulled from the external application and placed in a main queue in the case management application, where supervisors can assign out mail to specific users. These users would review the mail in this "raw" state before choosing to intake the mail. The review might involve requesting that a supervisor perform a specific action on the mail, such as merging it with another piece of mail, or editing certain details of the mail document.

Component Two

Component Two focuses on the intake of this mail document.

If the mail document is a specific type of form, it can be intaken as a case. Any other type of mail can be intaken as a mail document, which is called the mail intake process. If needed, a user going through the mail intake process can add tasks to the mail, link related mail, and link related cases.

Component Three

Component Three focuses on the post-intake process of the mail document.

Once the intake process is done, a piece of mail becomes recorded in the case manangement system. If tasks were added during the intake process, then the mail is still in a active state until that task is closed out. The piece of mail can still be interacted with post-intake; new tasks can be added and relationships between the mail and other pieces of mail or cases can be edited.

Reflections

This was my first time in a UX lead role, and my first time working with the development team involved in this project. Having worked with a very different development team in the past, I had certain expectations that were not met with this new team and communication was a huge hurdle because of that. I quickly learned that, at this point in my career, I was not only not ready to lead a project of this scope and tumultuousness but that I also needed to work on my communication skills and to work on establishing a process to my design work.

Through the obstacles presented by this project, I have learned to ask more questions to developers and work with them to establish a process that works for both of us. My deliverables have shifted depending on what development team I'm working with to fit their needs as closely as possible. And I have slowly started to develop an understanding of how to speak in developer terms. This project was half way through development when it was put on hold but I have learned from the experience immensely and have been able to apply what I've learned to other projects that I have worked on since then.

Challenges faced

  • Last minute changes from stakeholders, albeit minor, kept the solutioning team pivoting and on our toes.
  • Working with multiple designers at a time in the same file, while mananging communication issues during PTO and other events for all designers.

Solution

Expand on existing functionality by adding new request and review pathways for the addition, removal, and modification of issues on cases.

Mapping out the user flow

We approached the requirements gathering phase by meeting up with the stakeholders, getting a better understanding of the ask, and then exploring the existing functionality internally amongst ourselves. From there, we continued with our weekly cadence of stakeholder meetings to continue asking more questions and presenting initial wireframes until we were able to flesh both requirements and wireframes out enough. Common practice during this solutioning effort is creating a process flow deliverable, as seen below:

Admin Flow

The admin users identified during stakeholder meetings would be the users that review an issue addition, modification, and removal request for a specific case. These users would go into the typical team queue, which would have a new column that holds these requests. From there, the admins can click on a specific request and review the proposed changes made by a regular user. A case with more than one request could not be moved into the completed stage of the case lifecycle until all requests were reviewed (either approved or rejected) by an admin user. If a claim had several requests on it, each one could potentially be reviewed by different admin users.

Non-Admin Flow

The non-admin users identified during stakeholder meetings would be the users that are reviewing the claims on a daily basis. If deemed necessary, these users would be the ones to submit an issue addition, modiication, or removal request on a specific case that was in a completed status. Case that were in an "in progress" state could not have requests added to them by non-admin users, though admin users could still come in and add, remove, or modify issues in the regular manner (no request system). An additional use case did exist for non-admin users, where they could go into a claim with pending requests and add additional requests. A read-only state had to be established for prior requests that were submitted by someone other than the visiting user, while still allowing that visting user to:

  • Add new requests onto the claim
  • Edit their previous requests on the claim, if any
  • Cancel their previous request on the claim, if any

Reflections

I was the main point of contact for the UX design team for the majority of this project's lifecycle. However, this project did shift hands quite a bit within the UX team, and for a good portion of it I worked alongside three other designers. Having to work with multiple designers was not entirely a new experience for me, but it was still a learning experience with pros and cons. I learned a bit more about my own process watching other designers work, and what things I could do to not only improve my own design methods and efficienct but also how to better communicate with developers during handoff. One of the things that was introduced to me in this effort was the idea of annotated designs, where as UX designers we could mark and lay down notes on design specs and functionality that we wanted to highlight for the developers. Ever since then, I have been using this method of communication with developers and have expanded upon it by making it more of a dev guide, providing content outlines that are interactable for easy navigation. However, too many moving hands can also cause issues, such as miscommunication in the design process and preferences being put above others. I learned to express what I was and was not comfortable with in terms of work methods, and establish boundaries with my teammates, such as not wanting to push last minute changes before I was about to present.

Challenges faced

  • Multiple stakeholders with conflicting opinions, all needed to be weighed somewhat equally.
  • Working with a new development team that works a bit differently than other dev teams worked with in the past.
  • Effort transitioning to another development team, which caused some confusion and delays in the development process

Solution

Allow for decision evaluations to be revisted by judges and other authorized users through both existing and new pages in the application. Implement solutions used previously in another effort to prevent two users from editing the same evaluation at the same time. Allow for past evaluations to be viewed but not editable if it meets certain criteria, so as not to cause duplication of data.

From there, we continued with our weekly cadence of stakeholder meetings to continue asking more questions and presenting initial wireframes until we were able to flesh both requirements and wireframes out enough. Entry points for Decision Evaluations

The entry points for the decision evaluations were established during several rounds of the stakeholder meetings. Originally, the main entry points were going to be siloed to the case details page of applicable cases as well as the search page, but after further internal discussion we proposed a new view on the judges queue to the stakeholders. This new view would allow judges to quickly access and see all decision evaluations that had been submitted just by them, organized by attorney name. These three starting points would all lead to the same decision evaluation page, with slight differences to account for as seen in the overall user flow below:

Case Details

Under more research, the entry point of the case details page was determined to not be the main entry point taken by judges or authorized users. However, stakeholder's original request did include having this entry point and it was requested to remain even after several presentations. It would still prove useful as an entry point in the use case where a judge would be reviewing multiple cases for the same veteran, for example. The case details page is also a page with heavy traffic, given the amount of crucial information displayed on it, so it could often be opened for judges and other users to have a reference. In reality, this is more of a sub entry point, given that it is mainly accessed by the other two entry points of the judge queue and the search page.

Judge Queue

Every user in the application has a personal queue, where they can see cases assigned to them in different statuses. This effort would provide a new view in a judge's queue, where they can see decision evaluations that they have submitted and are both still editable or have passed the editable date and are read-only. Each case would be organized by attorney name, as well as other filterable pieces of information such as different score types and the date the evaluation was submitted. A search bar would allow for searching by fields that are not already filterable in the queue table, such as fields with unique values. The ability to download the queue table as a CSV file would also be available. This new view would be the main entry point for judges, unless they need to look for cases that have been submitted by other judges. Although initally seen as a business need, this entry point was later deprioritized and deemed out of scope given that it wasn't captured in the original draft of business requirements.

Search Page

The search page enhancement was three-fold. Firstly, Decision Evaluations would be added in as a possible result when specific users search existing search criteria on the existing search page. These same users would be able to search for decision evaluations using new search criteria introduced in a second phase of this search effort. Lastly, a potential third phase was discussed with the business to introduce new search criteria for the other two existing types of results, cases and reviews.

Reflections

Unlike the previous project, this project was handed off to three different development teams before it got properly worked on. The project was divided into two parts: Development team A would spearhead the case details entry point while development team B would take charge of the search page entry point. The initial development team that I had worked with did not properly document business requirements due to lack of frontend experience, so I and the leads of the different development teams had to work together on drafting business requirements. There were a few gaps missed during initial presentation of wireframes that had to be addressed during a later point in the development phase, but most of these gaps were linked to edge cases. Otherwise, the development process went off without many issues and deployed succesfully to UAT. Still, this experience made me realize that I have to really work on documenting not just rare edge cases and work on addressing them sooner rather than later, but I also have to work on vocalizing concerns and questioning how efforts are written up, even if it falls out of range of my immediate work responsibilities.
